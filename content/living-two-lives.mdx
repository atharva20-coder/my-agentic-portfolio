## The Version of Me at Home

At home, I'm the guy who sits at the dining table for dinner every night at 8. I help with groceries. I fix the WiFi router when it acts up. I know all my neighbors by name. My mom makes chai exactly the way I like it.

And then there's the version of me in Bangalore.

The one who wakes up at 11, skips meals when a feature is breaking, spends evenings on calls with founders and engineers, and has built an AI system that calls people on the phone and negotiates with them in real-time.

These two people share the same body and the same PAN card. But they live completely different lives.

## Why I Left

The honest reason: I couldn't build what I wanted to build from home.

Not because of internet speed or electricity. But because of context. When you're around people who don't know what a webhook is, explaining what you're working on starts to feel exhausting. And after a while, you stop explaining. And when you stop explaining, you start second-guessing whether it's even real.

Bangalore fixes that. You sit in a Koramangala café and the person next to you is building a B2B SaaS. The barista has a startup idea. Your auto driver once asked me if I'd heard of Sequoia.

It's not that Bangalore has better people. It's that the density of people who think the same way reaches some critical threshold — and that threshold changes what feels possible.

## The Decision to Build ArisX

I didn't wake up one morning and decide to build a voice AI company for BFSI. That's not how it happened.

I was working on automations, playing around with ElevenLabs, building a Goggins chatbot as a side project, then getting obsessed with MCP servers, then building JARVIS for Hinge. Each thing led to the next. The thread connecting all of it was: **I'm fascinated by AI that can have a real conversation.**

At some point I realized: this isn't just fun. Collections teams, procurement teams, lenders — they all do the same calls every day, hundreds of times. Introduction, verify identity, state the purpose, handle the objection, close. That's a script. And if it's a script, an AI can run it.

So I started building.

## What "Building" Actually Looks Like

Most people imagine founders in some loft with a whiteboard and a vision deck. My reality was:

- A rented flat in HSR Layout with one good chair and bad lighting
- Claude Code open on one monitor, a call going on the other
- Days where I shipped nothing because I went down a rabbit hole on audio buffer sizes
- Evenings where I'd figure something out and feel like I'd cracked gravity
- Nights where nothing worked and I genuinely questioned everything

The hardest part of early-stage building isn't technical. It's the silence. No team yet, no users yet, just you and the blinking cursor.

But Bangalore made that silence more bearable. You could step outside and find someone who'd been through the same thing. That matters more than I expected.

## Meeting YC-backed Leadership

At some point, Aria started working well enough that I could show it to real people.

I got a call with the CEO of a YC-backed company. I demo'd Aria — an outbound call where the AI handled a collections script, dealt with objections, maintained context across turns, and closed the interaction. Sub-600ms response time. Sounds like a trained rep.

They were genuinely interested.

What followed was a series of meetings with founders and leadership I'd only read about before. Not many 22-year-olds get to sit across from people who've taken companies from 0 to scale, and have them ask *you* questions about your tech.

I won't pretend that wasn't surreal.

## The Duality Is Real

Every time I go home — and I do go home, as often as I can — there's a gear shift.

In Bangalore, I'm moving fast. Every day has leverage. I'm talking to investors, debugging production calls, thinking about TTFT latency and Gemini's session compression.

At home, I'm watching cricket with my dad. Having lunch that takes an hour because nobody's rushing. Sleeping at a normal human time.

And weirdly — both are necessary.

The Bangalore version of me would burn out without the hometown reset. And the hometown version of me would go soft without the Bangalore pressure.

Living two lives isn't a problem to solve. It's the actual texture of building something while staying human.

## What I've Learned About Cities

Cities don't make you smart. They don't hand you opportunity.

What they do is change your baseline for what's normal. In my hometown, building a startup is "risky." In Bangalore, not building something is what requires explaining.

That shift in baseline — what feels possible, what feels ambitious, what feels like the obvious next move — is worth more than any accelerator program or how-to-build-a-startup article.

## Right Now

Aria is in production. Real BFSI and NBFC clients. Real calls. Real outcomes.

ArisX is a platform now — voice bots and WhatsApp agents running the same collection and outreach workflows, 24/7, at a fraction of the cost of human agents.

We're not at scale yet. But we're no longer a demo. That line — from demo to production — is the one that actually matters, and we crossed it.

I'm still in Bangalore. Still going home when I can. Still figuring out how to be both versions of myself without either one disappearing.

It's a strange way to live. I wouldn't trade it.
